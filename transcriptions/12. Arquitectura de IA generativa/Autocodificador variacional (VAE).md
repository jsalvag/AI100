can give you an enormous amount of flexibility,
but you've already seen that sometimes you don't need
all that power.
This is particularly true if you want to colorize an old image
or increase its resolution.
For that, you might want to try an autoencoder.
Autoencoders are the silent work engine
from many of the AI image tools that you'll use today.
They'll use supervised or unsupervised machine learning
to come up with a pattern of perfection.
They can be trained with supervised or unsupervised learning
to look through millions of images of similar subjects.
Then the autoencoder will encode all these images
into perfect versions of the subject.
So if your photograph contains a vase,
then the system will have encoded the essence of the vase.
Then this essence of tens of thousands of vases
can improve the picture of your imperfect vase.
In a sense, they try to encode the essence of an image
and then decode any new images
to match this pattern of perfection.
So say you have an image of a tree
that's from an old photograph,
autoencoders can make it look like a modern photograph
by sharpening and even colorizing the image.
The most popular autoencoder
is the variational autoencoder.
These encoders can look for patterns and images,
let's say tree images.
Then it will cluster together the essence
of these tree images.
This process is called encoding.
It extracts the tree from a latent space.
You can almost imagine it like a pencil outline of a tree.
Then the artificial neural network
can use this pencil outline or code
to enhance or even generate new tree images.
That means that the encoder will be able
to separate the tree from the old scratches or grainy noise.
Then it can remove that noise
and produce a much clear image of a tree.
In a sense, the system is separating out the signal,
which is the tree, from the noise, which is all the parts
of the image that are not the tree.
For a variational autoencoder,
all the non-tree items in the image are called anomalies.
That's why part of the encoding is called anomaly detection.
You're detecting the parts of the photo
that are not the tree, and then filtering them out.
Once you have that pure tree code, you can use the system
to decode the image as a perfect copy
or create one that's been dramatically improved.
Now, autoencoders do suffer from some of the challenges
of traditional predictive AI.
These systems can only create perfect trees
or vases if it's already been trained on thousands
or even tens of thousands of labeled images.
Plus, it doesn't have the flexibility you get
from a foundation model.
You wouldn't be able to generate a vase with a tree inside.
But for bread-and-butter image enhancement,
autoencoders are a very powerful way to improve your images.
It's always fun to generate new images,
but many photographers, artists and graphic designers
are just looking to enhance the images they already have.
So even though this technique is a form of predictive AI,
it's still plenty useful for anyone who works
with digital images.
