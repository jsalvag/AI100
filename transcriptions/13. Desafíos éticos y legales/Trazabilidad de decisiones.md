- You've seen that sometimes AI systems
do what you say but not what you want.
This value alignment can cause a lot of difficulties,
especially in complex AI systems,
but that's not the only problem you'll run into
with machine learning algorithms.
Sometimes the system will do what you say,
but you'll have no idea how it made the decision.
This challenge is often referred to
as decision traceability.
It's when the steps the system took
to arrive at a decision become really difficult
for humans to understand.
Imagine again that you work for a health insurance company.
Your company created an AI system that would decide
which medical procedures should be covered.
To build the system,
your company gathered enormous amounts of data.
This is a predictive AI system that would look
through past medical procedures
and find patterns in the best results.
To be effective, this system would have to look
through millions of different medical procedures.
Then it would have to look at each of the results.
If a procedure has a low success rate,
the AI system wouldn't recommend it for future patients.
Now, imagine that a patient calls up
your health insurance company
and asks if their procedure is covered.
The AI system determines that the procedure's not effective,
and so it's not covered for this patient.
Now, what happens if the patient asks
why the procedure isn't covered?
How would the representative answer that question?
Remember that this predictive AI system made this decision
based on enormous amounts of data.
Then it saw patterns about which procedures
had the highest success rate.
These patterns would be almost impossible
for any human to identify.
In some ways, this AI system is like a black box.
The customer representative puts in a request
and then the system outputs an answer.
The more complex the system, the more difficult it is
for any human to be able to look into that black box.
There's no way to make sense
of all those decisions and data.
This problem has become so common that some AI researchers
have started developing what's called explainable AI or XAI.
These systems try to do a high-level explanation
for the AI decisions,
but it can't always interpret what the machine sees.
Some decisions are simply unexplainable.
Of course, as these systems become more complex,
it becomes much more difficult to explain.
This is especially true with newer generative AI systems
that rely on massive foundation models.
These systems are so complex
that even the people who develop them are uncertain
what would happen if you made changes
to the underlying foundation model.
In many ways, decision traceability isn't really solvable.
Complex AI systems are simply better than humans
at finding patterns in enormous datasets.
Having the system interpret its decision
is almost like trying to explain algebra to a cat,
and yes, in this example, we're the cats.
But for many systems, especially in healthcare,
humans need to be in the loop.
When you rely on these systems,
it's important that humans can have some idea
of the important steps the machine took
to make this decision.
