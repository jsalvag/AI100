- There's no doubt that artificial intelligence
is a highly technical field.
You've seen that even the earliest forms of AI
relied on computers, data, and programming.
So it makes sense to think that the challenges
that you'll run into will also be technical.
But the truth is much more complicated.
In fact, some of AI's challenges will lead
to deep moral questions.
You should consider your responsibilities to users
and how they align with your organization's values.
It's not just about asking interesting moral questions.
It's a key part of working with AI systems.
The chance of your work being thrown away
because of ethical problems is probably far greater
than the chance of some technical issue.
In AI, the main ethical challenges are:
value alignment, algorithmic traceability,
copyright, and reliability,
like generative AI hallucinations.
The first big challenge is value alignment.
In many ways, this challenge is probably the most familiar.
That's because it's the subject
of so many science fiction stories.
It's when the killer robots decide
that their biggest threat is humanity,
or it's when the AI in "2001: A Space Odyssey"
kills the crew to fulfill its mission.
It's about the system doing what you said,
but not what you wanted.
The alignment problem occurs
when you create an AI system to perform a task.
Then you assume that it will think and reason like a human.
In a sense, it's when the AI system does what you tell it,
but not in a way a human would expect.
Imagine working for a health insurance company.
Your team developed an AI system to reduce costs.
You rewarded the system
whenever it found a high-cost customer.
That AI system would happily cancel all the policies
for anybody over the age of 50,
since older customers need more healthcare.
That would certainly lower your costs,
but it would also leave
some of your longest-paying customers without insurance.
The system did what you asked, but not what you wanted.
It worked to maximize its reward
without thinking about any of the moral questions.
Many of the most vocal opponents
of artificial intelligence are worried
about AI systems gaining some form of consciousness.
But the reality is that systems are plenty dangerous
with just a little bit of competence.
With a little bit of confidence,
an AI system can make a lot of decisions.
As systems make more decisions,
there's a danger that they become misaligned
with human values.
A competent AI system can make a lot of moral decisions
before anyone notices.
In fact, it might be difficult for humans to detect
when these systems are doing something that's immoral.
Most of the time, it's just working towards a reward.
It'll be up to humans to go back through
and make sure that they're aligned with human values.
This is especially true when the objectives are uncertain.
To reduce health insurance costs,
the AI system needs to recognize
the importance of not excluding older customers.
If it understands these values,
then it can apply them to your future objectives.
That way, you can still give the system power
to make decisions, but at the same time,
avoid unexpected outcomes.
You don't want your customer or even society
to suffer because of a simple misalignment.
